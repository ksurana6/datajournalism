Most asylum-seekers in the EU aren't allowed to stay, but that doesn't mean they go home.

This past summer almost 200,000 people crossed the Mediterranean from North Africa to reach Italy's shores. While some take the trip hoping for better economic opportunities, many of the newcomers are fleeing war and violence in their home countries. The UN High Commission on Refugees estimates that 25% of the arrivals were from war-torn Syria, and another 25% fled the oppresive military dictatorship in Eritrea.

(notes In 2013 there were 398,234 asylum applications and 326,585 asylum application decisions. 48,000 people arrived to Italy, but 27,832 applied for asylum. Not direct correlation but shows something is up)

But how is Europe handling the influx of asylum seekers? EU countries are obliged to recognize valid claims for asylum under the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees and its 1967 Protocol. As defined by these agreements, a refugee is a person who is outside his or her country of nationality (or place of habitual residence if stateless) who, owing to a fear of persecution on account of a protected ground, is unable or unwilling to avail himself of the protection of the state. Protected grounds include race, nationality, religion, political opinion and membership of a particular social group. The signatories to these agreements are further obliged not to return or "refoul" refugees to the place where they would face persecution.

Meanwhile, Afghanis, Iraqis, Iranians and people from Serbia and Kosovo have a preference for Germany. France receives the most applications from former colony Democratic Republic of the Congo, but not Algeria, and receives more applicants from Bangladesh and Albania than other countries.

Strangely, Italy comes up as a top destination only for Nigerians. Spain, Greece and Bulgaria barely register in the asylum application numbers. This is interesting because Italy is the primary portal through which migrants and refugees reach Europe. The other border countries, like Spain, Greece and Bulgaria, also receive many migrants. Under EU law, asylum-seekers should be fingerprinted and apply for refugee status in the first EU country they reach. Clearly this is not happening. But why?
